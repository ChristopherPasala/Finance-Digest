Financial Glossary

Plain-English definitions for the financial terms used throughout this site.

Beta
A measure of a stock's volatility relative to the market. A beta above 1 means more volatile than the market; below 1 means less volatile.
CAGR
Compound Annual Growth Rate — the rate at which something grows over multiple years as if it grew at a steady rate each year. Used to compare growth across different time periods.
Cash Conversion Ratio
Operating cash flow divided by net profit — measures how effectively a company converts reported earnings into actual cash. A ratio above 1 suggests high-quality earnings; below 1 may indicate aggressive accounting or working capital issues.
CapEx
Capital Expenditure — money spent by a company to acquire or maintain long-term physical assets like factories, equipment, or property.
DCF
Discounted Cash Flow — a valuation method that estimates what a company is worth today based on its expected future cash flows, adjusted for the time value of money.
EBIT
Earnings Before Interest and Taxes — a measure of a company's operating profitability before the effects of financing (interest) and tax decisions. A higher EBIT indicates the core business generates more profit from its operations. A lower or negative EBIT signals the business is struggling to cover its operating costs, regardless of how it is financed.
EBITDA
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortisation — a measure of a company's core operational profitability, stripping out financing and accounting decisions.
EPS
Earnings Per Share — a company's net profit divided by the number of outstanding shares. A higher EPS generally indicates greater profitability per share.
EV/EBITDA
Enterprise Value divided by EBITDA — a valuation multiple used to compare companies regardless of their debt levels or accounting differences. Lower usually means cheaper.
FCF Yield
Free Cash Flow divided by market cap — shows how much free cash flow is generated for every dollar invested. Higher yield suggests better value.
FCF
Free Cash Flow — the cash a company generates after paying for capital expenditure. A key indicator of financial health and the ability to pay dividends or buy back shares.
Forward P/E
Price-to-Earnings ratio calculated using projected future earnings rather than past earnings. Reflects what the market expects the company to earn going forward.
Gross Margin
Revenue minus the cost of goods sold, expressed as a percentage. Shows how efficiently a company produces its products before accounting for operating expenses.
MACD
Moving Average Convergence Divergence — a technical indicator tracking the relationship between two moving averages of a stock's price, used to spot momentum shifts.
Market Cap
Market Capitalisation — the total market value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated as share price multiplied by number of shares. Determines company size (small, mid, large cap).
Moat
A durable competitive advantage that protects a company from competition, like a strong brand, patents, network effects, or switching costs. Companies with wide moats tend to sustain profits longer.
Net Debt
Total borrowings minus cash and equivalents. A negative net debt means the company holds more cash than it owes — a sign of financial strength.
Operating Margin
Operating income as a percentage of revenue — measures how much profit a company earns from its core business before paying interest and taxes.
P/E
Price-to-Earnings ratio — the share price divided by earnings per share. Shows how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of earnings. A high P/E may indicate growth expectations or overvaluation.
ROE
Return on Equity — net income divided by shareholders' equity. Measures how effectively management uses equity capital to generate profit. A consistently high ROE (above 15%) often signals a durable competitive advantage.
ROIC
Return on Invested Capital — measures how efficiently a company turns invested capital into profit. A ROIC above the cost of capital means the business is creating value.
RSI
Relative Strength Index — a momentum indicator on a scale of 0 to 100. Readings below 30 suggest the stock may be oversold (potential buy signal); above 70 suggests overbought (potential sell signal).
SMA
Simple Moving Average — the average closing price of a stock over a specific number of days (e.g. 50-day SMA). Used to smooth out price data and identify trends.
